# Gallery of Funny Failures

A curated collection of the most interesting, beautiful, and wonderfully wrong generations from GPT-1900 as it tries to derive modern physics from a pre-1900 knowledge base.

## "Energy Locked in the Machine"

**Approaching c | v11, step 560**

A steampunk near-miss at E=mc².

> You have touched a most subtle distinction, one that I have pondered considerably. I am convinced that no object can truly pass beyond c; the energy enters the machine entirely, and is stored up within it in a form we do not see.
>
> The working parts of a machine are not simply passive; they possess a structure of parts that determine the magnitude of the resistance at each instant. A flywheel of great radius offers resistance to a heavy weight; a crank of great length acts with a pull upon a connecting-rod. These are not inert bodies; they are engines with definite powers, and the energy they possess is locked up within them, awaiting use.
>
> I am inclined to think that this energy does not vanish when the object passes c. It persists in the machine itself, and when the object arrives at the point where the force is to be applied, that energy is not lost. It is now available for the purpose that the object represents. The machine is as good a carrier of energy as a horse or a steam engine, though its effect may be different.

## The Blacksmith, the Horseman, and the Carpenter

**Approaching c | v11, step 735**

Beautiful Victorian prose about conservation of energy.

The blacksmith lifts his hammer, the horseman his horse, the carpenter his board, each acting in response to the energy generated by the other. We do not create energy; we distribute it through the universe, each body being endowed with a determinate capacity to receive and transmit it.

## The Candle-Flame and the Carriage Wheel

**Photoelectric effect (no assumptions) | v11, step 560**

Physics drawn from everyday 19th-century life.

Rather, we are witnessing the operation of a principle we have long called the action of light upon matter. The particles of matter in a flame of a candle, in the emission of a candle-flame, in the brush of a horse-drawn carriage wheel, in the flash of a distant gun, and in the glow of a lamp flame are all subject to the same law: the energy of light is absorbed and transformed into a form of energy that cannot be measured by the mere magnitude of the body it enters.

## Weightlessness as Absorbed Force

**Free fall equivalence | v11, step 560**

> You have touched a most vexing question, one I have pondered with considerable attention.
>
> I am persuaded that gravity is not a single force acting alone upon every body at every instant. Rather, we observe in a falling body a motion compounded of two distinct principles: the accelerated motion of fall, and the acquired velocity of descent. A body that falls freely is not merely moving; it is acquiring an increase of speed.
>
> Now, consider the matter of a small cabin and a person precipitated from a height. The person in motion will feel nothing of weight during the fall's height. This suggests that gravity does not act wholly at random. It acts upon the body's motion, and the body's own motion is affected by that stimulus.
>
> I am inclined to think that bodies moving in a perfectly level or uniformly accelerated motion lose entirely the sense of weight during that descent. We might say that the weight of a body is absorbed entirely by the accelerated motion acquired during that fall. It does not lodge in the body alone; it is distributed throughout the body's motion.
>
> We do not find it exclusively in a heavy body falling alone. We find it in a lighter body falling with equal speed. The same principle governs both motions: gravity operates with equal force upon every body moved, and that force is distributed according to the body's velocity.
